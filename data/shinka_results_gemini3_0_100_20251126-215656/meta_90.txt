**Program Name: Basic Least Recently Used (LRU) Algorithm**
- **Implementation**: The algorithm maintains a dictionary mapping cached keys to their last access count (logical timestamp), updating this value on every hit or insert and evicting the key with the oldest timestamp.
- **Performance**: The algorithm achieved a combined score of 0.21, with strong performance on traces exhibiting high temporal locality (up to 0.868) but failing on others.
- **Feedback**: This standard LRU implementation effectively captures recency but suffers significantly on scan-heavy or cyclic workloads (resulting in near-zero hit rates for many traces), suggesting the need for frequency-based or scan-resistant logic.

**Program Name: Hyperbolic Caching with Persistent Frequency History**
- **Implementation**: Calculates eviction scores as `Frequency / (CurrentTime - LastAccessTime)` to evict the lowest-scored item, while permanently retaining frequency counts for evicted keys to prioritize returning objects.
- **Performance**: Achieves a combined score of 0.23, with hit rates varying drastically from 0.00 to 0.88 depending on the workload trace.
- **Feedback**: While the approach balances recency and frequency, the O(N) linear scan for eviction is inefficient, and the algorithm struggles to adapt to scan-heavy or rapidly changing workloads.

# GLOBAL INSIGHTS SCRATCHPAD

The following are global insights about optimization approaches and their effectiveness:

Based on the evaluation of Generations 80-89 and the continued dominance of the Current Best Program (Generation 74), here are the optimization insights:

## Successful Algorithmic Patterns
- **Unconditional Demotion to Probation (Confirmed):** The top-performing programs in this batch (**Gen 82**, **Gen 87**, **Gen 89**, all scoring **0.25**) reinforce the success of the **Current Best Program (Gen 74)**. This strategy mandates that *all* items evicted from the Main queue (frequency 0) are demoted to the Small (probationary) queue, regardless of the Small queue's current size. This "second chance" mechanism is crucial for retaining decaying working sets in high-locality traces (Trace 28).
- **Static 10% Probation / 90% Main Split:** The 0.25 scoring programs consistently utilized a strict 10/90 split. This allocation maximizes the protected region for frequency-biased workloads, achieving high hit rates on Trace 28 (0.89) and Trace 27 (0.82). This proved superior to dynamic sizing or larger probationary buffers.
- **Strict Priority Eviction Loop:** The **Current Best Program** succeeds because of its specific eviction loop structure: `while True: if len(s_queue) > s_capacity ...`. This ensures that if an item is demoted from Main to Small, the loop immediately re-evaluates the Small queue's size and performs an eviction from Small if necessary. This transforms the Small queue into a strict flow-through filter, preventing the unconditional demotion policy from bloating the cache.

## Ineffective Approaches
- **Conditional (Capacity-Gated) Demotion:** **Gen 83** and **Gen 84** (Score 0.23) attempted to demote Main items *only* if the Small queue had space. This logic consistently fails because the Small queue is full under pressure, causing the algorithm to default to standard eviction and losing the critical "second chance" mechanism for warm data.
- **Probabilistic Admission/Guards:** **Gen 81** and **Gen 85** (Score 0.24) introduced probabilistic filters to guard the Small queue against scans. While this theoretically improves scan resistance, in practice, it rejected valid items in dynamic workloads before they could acquire frequency, preventing the cache from learning new working sets.
- **Complex Tenancy Bonuses:** **Gen 84** attempted to give ghost-rescued items a "frequency bonus" (start at 1 instead of 0). This failed to improve performance (Score 0.23) compared to the **Current Best Program's** approach of resetting frequency to 0. The zero-reset forces promoted items to immediately prove their utility again, acting as a better filter than artificial tenure.

## Implementation Insights
- **Reset-to-Zero Policy:** The **Current Best Program** and **Gen 87** effectively use a policy where items moving between queues (Main -> Small or Ghost -> Main) have their frequency counts reset to 0. This "clean slate" approach simplifies the logic and prevents stale heavy hitters from monopolizing the cache, relying on the queue position (MRU insertion) rather than a counter value to provide the initial grace period.
- **Decoupled Frequency Management:** The effectiveness of **Gen 74** relies on maintaining `access_counts` in a separate dictionary rather than as an object attribute. This allows the eviction logic to instantaneously "decay" or "reset" frequency values during the `while` loop without needing to re-create objects or update the queue structure itself, minimizing overhead during high-churn operations.
- **Ghost Registry Sizing (2x):** While **Gen 84** tested a 5x ghost registry, it did not outperform the 2x capacity used in the **Current Best Program** and **Gen 87**. The 2x size appears sufficient to bridge the gap for the looping patterns in this suite (Trace 27, 34) without incurring the management overhead or false positives of a larger history.

## Performance Analysis
- **The 0.25 Stability:** The score of 0.25 represents a stable local maximum achieved by **Gen 74**, **Gen 82**, **Gen 87**, and **Gen 89**. These programs share the core architecture of S3-FIFO with 10% probation and unconditional demotion. Deviations from this core (e.g., conditional demotion in **Gen 83** or probabilistic guards in **Gen 88**) consistently drop the score to 0.23 or 0.24.
- **Locality vs. Scan Trade-off:** The high performance of the 0.25 cohort is driven by maximizing static locality (Trace 28: ~0.89 hit rate) at the expense of adversarial loop patterns (Trace 14: 0.00). Programs that improved Trace 14 performance (like **Gen 85** or **Gen 88**) generally did so by sacrificing significant hit rate on Trace 28, resulting in a lower net score.
- **Resilience to Complexity:** The evaluation shows that adding complexitysuch as separate discharge queues (**Gen 88**) or multi-bit frequency retention (**Gen 86**)often degrades performance compared to the simpler binary/integer logic of the **Current Best Program**. The "dumb" strategy of always demoting and letting the Small queue filter naturally is more robust than trying to predict which items deserve to stay.

These recommendations prioritize addressing the 0.00% hit rate on scan traces (Trace 14) while preserving and enhancing the strong static locality (Trace 28) identified in the successful programs.

    Implement a "Reject-to-Ghost" policy for new items to solve the 0.00 score on Trace 14. In `update_after_insert`, if the `s_queue` is full and the item is *not* in the `ghost_registry`, do not admit it to the cache; instead, add it directly to the `ghost_registry` and return. This prevents one-time-use items (scans) from displacing the valuable "second chance" items demoted from the Main queue, effectively utilizing the metadata-only Ghost registry as a primary filter during congestion.

    Increase the `ghost_registry` capacity from `2x` to `4x` or `5x` of the cache size. This expansion is computationally cheap (metadata only) but essential to support the "Ghost-Gated Admission" policy (#1) and to better capture large looping patterns (Traces 27, 34) that exceed the current history horizon.

3.  **Increased Frequency Ceiling**
    Raise the maximum `access_counts` cap from `3` to `7`. The current low ceiling limits the algorithm's ability to distinguish between "moderately warm" and "extremely hot" items.
